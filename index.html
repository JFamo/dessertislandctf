Mixing and matching which extrons are included happens naturally. Dropping an extron here or there intentionally is called Alternative Splicing, and you can read more about it here.

However, when there are mutations that affect where splicing takes place, sections may be included/dropped that should not have been included/dropped.

That sounds bad - What happens as a result?

When a mutation occurs in DNA, this may affect the splice locations of the pre-mRNA made from that DNA. As a result, incorrect mRNA, and therefore incorrect proteins, are produced. This leads to a long list of potential genetic diseases:

  • Familial isolated Growth Hormone Deficiency type II
  • Wilms Tumours - pediatric kidney cancer
  • Denys-Drash syndrome
  • Fraisier syndrome
  • Cystic Fibrosis
  • Retinis Pigmentosa - progressive retinal degernation, affects 1 in 4000 people
  • Spinal muscular atrophy
  • And more
  • Source

Note - A single cell with a mutation will not necesarily lead to the above - Inheriting mutated DNA, however, propogates the mutation throughout the entire body. This, in some cases, is bad.

This is Outrageous! Who's responsible?

As seen above, mutations in DNA that are passed on to the next generation have the capability of causing problems.

What can I do to avoid this?

Let's trace the chain of events backwards, and re-phrase the question:

  • The diseases above are caused by incorrect proteins
  • Incorrect proteins are made by ribosomes processing incorrect mRNA
  • Incorrect mRNA comes from incorrect splicing of pre-mRNA
  • Incorrect splicing happens due to mutations in DNA
  • Mutations in DNA are caused mainly by environmental factors ( Source )

What environmental factors should I avoid, to prevent passing on mutated DNA to future generations?

In conclusion, to avoid mutations in DNA that could put future generations at risk, One should avoid certain environmental factors. These mutation causing factors are commonly called Mutagens. Note that exposure does not guarantee mutation - X rays are very common, and most of us encounter UV rays from the sun every day. High exposure, or continued, prolonged exposure, is much more reasonable to be concerned about.

Some mutagens to avoid:

  • Ionizing Radiation (Such as X-Rays)
  • Low-Energy Radiation (UV light)
  • Tobacco smoke (Even second hand [ Source ])
  • Radon [ Source ]
  • Asbestos [ Source ]

The topic of chemical mutagens is very complex. For more reading, check here and here.
